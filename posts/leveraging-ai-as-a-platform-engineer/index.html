I have been using AI coding assistants seriously for about a year. Not casually, not experimentally: as a core part of how I do my job every day. I work across a wide surface area, writing Go and Python, managing cloud infrastructure, debugging distributed systems, automating operational workflows. AI has changed how I approach all of it, but not in the ways the marketing copy says.

This is not a post about AI generating code. It is about how AI changes the way an experienced engineer thinks, investigates, and makes decisions.

The Shift That Actually Matters
#

The naive version of AI-assisted development is: you describe what you want, the AI writes it, you review and move on. This is real and useful, but it is also the least interesting thing AI does for me.

The more important shift is that AI makes investigation cheap. Before, when I hit an unfamiliar problem, I had to choose: spend an hour reading documentation and tracing code paths, or make an educated guess and iterate. With AI, investigation has near-zero friction. I can explore a codebase I have never seen, understand a system’s behavior from its logs, or reason about failure modes, in minutes instead of hours.

This changes the shape of my work. I investigate more, guess less. I read the error carefully before trying a fix. I ask “why” before asking “how to fix”.

The Investigation-First Pattern
#

The most consistent pattern I have developed is: before doing anything, investigate.

When something breaks, I do not go straight to a fix. I paste the error, the relevant logs, the surrounding context, and ask: what is actually happening here? The AI reasons through it, often surfacing the root cause faster than I would tracing it manually. Then we fix the right thing.

This sounds obvious but it is a meaningful behavioral change. It is easy to pattern-match on a familiar-looking error and jump to a solution that fixes the symptom. AI-assisted investigation makes it just as fast to find the actual cause, so I do that instead.

The same pattern applies to design decisions. Before writing code, I will often describe the problem and ask: how would you approach this? What are the tradeoffs? Are there options I am not considering? Not because I cannot think through it myself, but because doing it in conversation surfaces things I would have missed working alone.

Graduated Autonomy
#

One of the more interesting dynamics is how I grant authority to AI tools differently depending on the task.

For exploratory work, I am specific: “inspect this, do not change anything, just tell me what you find.” For well-understood implementation, I am open: “yes, go ahead and implement it.” For strategic decisions, I want a proposal first: “what would you do here?” followed by a decision, followed by “proceed.”

The pattern looks something like this:

Exploration → "check", "inspect", "analyze", "find" (read-only)
     ↓
Proposal   → "what do you think", "how would you approach this"
     ↓
Decision   → I approve, refine, or redirect
     ↓
Execution  → "ok", "yes", "proceed", "go ahead" (full autonomy)

The important thing is that each stage is distinct. I do not skip from “I have a problem” to “fix everything.” The proposal stage is where I catch bad assumptions before they become bad code.

Context Is Everything
#

The quality of AI output scales directly with the quality of context you provide. This is the thing that differentiates engineers who get a lot out of AI from those who are underwhelmed by it.

When I ask a question, I give the AI what it needs to answer it correctly:

  • The actual error message, not a paraphrase
  • The relevant code, not a description of what it does
  • The plan or spec document, pasted inline
  • The constraints: “this has to be backwards compatible”, “we cannot change the API contract”, “this runs on a 512MB Lambda”

A vague prompt produces a generic answer. A specific prompt with rich context produces a useful answer. This sounds trivial but in practice it means I spend a few extra seconds framing a question properly, and the difference in output quality is significant.

Chaining Tasks in a Session
#

AI coding assistants are most powerful when used as a sustained work session, not a series of one-off questions. A typical session for me looks like:

  1. Start with an investigation (“why is this service slow?”)
  2. AI finds something interesting (“the N+1 query in this handler”)
  3. I ask it to fix it (“ok, fix that”)
  4. AI makes the change, I review
  5. “Also, while we’re here, can you add an index on that column?”
  6. “And write a test for the fixed path”
  7. “Now commit and push”

Each step builds on the previous one. The AI has full context of what we changed and why. This kind of compounding is much more productive than treating each question as isolated.

The “also” and “and now” patterns are key. Once you have context established, extending the task costs almost nothing.

AI for Infrastructure, Not Just Code
#

One of the things that surprised me was how much value AI adds outside of writing application code.

Terraform, Kubernetes manifests, IAM policies, CI/CD pipelines: these are all just structured text with complex semantics. AI is as useful here as it is in application code, often more so, because the cost of mistakes is higher and the documentation is dense.

When I need to write a new IAM policy, I describe what the service needs to do and the AI produces a least-privilege policy. I read it, verify it, apply it. When a Terraform plan has unexpected changes, I paste the diff and ask what they mean. When a GitLab CI pipeline is failing, I paste the job log and ask what went wrong.

The pattern is the same: provide context, investigate, then act. The domain does not matter.

This extends to one-off operational tasks that I would otherwise script manually. Need to find all S3 buckets in an account that have public access enabled? Ask the AI to write the AWS CLI command. Need to parse a JSON log format to extract a specific metric? Give it a sample and ask for a jq expression. The barrier to “just do it properly” drops to near zero.

The Dry-Run Habit
#

For any task that touches production or makes irreversible changes, I have developed a consistent habit: ask for a dry-run first.

“What would you do if I asked you to rotate all the API keys for this service?”

The AI describes its plan. I check whether it understands the scope correctly, whether the sequence of operations makes sense, whether there are edge cases it has not considered. Then I say “ok, do it.”

This is not just about catching errors. It is about building trust in the AI’s judgment over time. If the dry-run plan looks right consistently, I become more confident granting full autonomy. If it misses something important, I know to be more explicit in my instructions.

# The same pattern applies when building agents:
# always implement a plan mode before execute mode

def run(task, dry_run: bool = False):
    plan = agent.plan(task)  # always plan first
    if dry_run:
        return plan           # return plan, no side effects
    return agent.execute(plan)

What Does Not Work
#

A few things that looked promising and did not pan out:

Asking for complete designs upfront. “Design a full authentication system for my application” produces a generic answer. “I have a FastAPI app, users are already in Keycloak, and I need to protect these three endpoints” produces something I can use. Specific problems get specific solutions.

Using AI as a rubber stamp. Asking the AI to review code you have already decided is fine produces polite agreement. Better to ask adversarially: “what could go wrong with this approach?”, “what am I missing?”, “is there a simpler way to do this?”

Long, elaborate prompts for simple tasks. Over-specifying a simple request produces over-engineered output. “Write a function that parses this JSON” works better than three paragraphs explaining the full system context when the function is straightforward.

Ignoring the AI’s questions. When the AI asks a clarifying question, it usually means the task is under-specified in a way that will produce a wrong answer. Answer the question rather than pushing through.

The Practical Reality
#

AI has not made me faster at writing code. It has made me faster at solving problems. The distinction matters. Writing code is rarely the bottleneck in software engineering. Understanding what to build, finding the right approach, debugging unexpected behavior, navigating unfamiliar systems: those are the hard parts, and AI makes all of them faster.

I also spend more time reading than I used to. When the AI produces code, I read it properly before committing it. When it gives a plan, I think about it before saying “proceed.” The value of AI is not in removing human judgment from the loop. It is in making human judgment more informed and faster to apply.

The engineers who get the most out of AI tools are not the ones who prompt the best. They are the ones who investigate before acting, provide real context, ask adversarial questions, and stay genuinely in the loop. AI is a force multiplier for the habits that make good engineers good, and a poor substitute for them.
